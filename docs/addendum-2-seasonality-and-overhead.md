# Addendum 2 — Annual seasonality and overhead recovery

> Supplements the main costing brief and Addendum 1. This one corrects a rule that is already in the brief.

---

## 1. The operating year is about ten months, not twelve

**There are no Umrah departures during the Hajj months — roughly two months of every year with zero departures and therefore zero pilgrim revenue.**

The company does not currently hold a **PIHK** licence, so Hajj itself is not a legal line of business for us. During that window the only thing we can do is place pilgrims with an association or with a travel operator that genuinely holds a PIHK permit.

Meanwhile **fixed obligations continue unchanged every month**: bank loan instalments, staff salaries, office operations, electricity, and the rest.

## 2. This makes the staff-salary allocation rule in the main brief wrong

The brief specifies: charge Rp1,000,000 per pilgrim when the month has a single departure, and nothing when it has two or more.

That rule implicitly assumes twelve productive months. With only about ten, **monthly allocation systematically under-recovers overhead by roughly 17%.** If the Rp1,000,000 figure was derived from a twelve-month view, the correct figure is closer to Rp1,200,000.

The shortfall does not surface as a loss on any individual departure. It surfaces as a **cash deficit during the Hajj months**, which is precisely why it is easy to miss — the departures all look profitable while the year quietly does not add up.

**Replace the rule with annual overhead absorption:**

- Maintain an **annual fixed-overhead pool**: salaries, bank instalments, office operations, utilities, and annual events.
- Divide by **expected pilgrims for the operating year**, not by the pilgrims in a given month.
- Apply the resulting per-pilgrim figure uniformly to every departure.
- Track **recovery to date against the annual pool**, so that under-absorption becomes visible during the year rather than at the end of it. Surface this as a running figure, not a year-end report.

This is simpler than the conditional monthly rule, more accurate, and it absorbs the dead months automatically instead of pretending they do not exist.

## 3. Annual events belong in the pool

The company holds an annual **halal bihalal** gathering that brings agents from across Indonesia together in Karawang. It is a substantial, entirely predictable annual cost covering venue, catering and logistics.

If it is not budgeted into the annual overhead pool it will keep appearing as an unexpected expense despite recurring every year. Include it, along with any other annual or semi-annual commitments of the same character.

## 4. Implications for the cash-flow module (Module 2)

The working-capital model must be **annual with a known revenue gap**, not a rolling monthly projection:

- The deepest cash trough of the year is **structural and predictable**: pilgrim settlements stop for roughly two months while fixed obligations continue, and deposits for post-Hajj departures may still be falling due inside that window.
- Because the timing is known in advance, the model should project the trough far enough ahead to be acted on rather than merely reported.
- Any placement fees earned from routing pilgrims to licensed PIHK operators during the Hajj window should be capturable as a partial offset, even if small.

## 5. Future milestone

**PIHK licensing** belongs alongside IATA accreditation as a business milestone that would change the model — it would convert the two dead months into an operating season. Do not build for it now, but avoid any design that hard-codes an assumption of Umrah-only operations or of a twelve-month departure calendar.
