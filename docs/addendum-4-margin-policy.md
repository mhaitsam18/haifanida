# Addendum 4 — Margin policy across package tiers

> Hand to Claude Code at the Phase 5 or Phase 6 gate. This is the margin floor policy previously owed; Phase 6's endgame panel needs it.

---

## 1. The floor is not one number

The Rp2,000,000 margin floor in the main brief was stated with the **standard package** in mind. It does not generalise. Each tier serves a different market with a different risk profile and a different volume, and the owner's policy differs accordingly.

| Tier | Typical publish | Margin policy | Typical pilgrims |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Budget / promo | Rp23,000,000–30,000,000 | Thin by design — a Rp900,000 projection has been accepted and gone ahead | Very high; 160 on one departure |
| Standard | ~Rp38,000,000 | Floor Rp2,000,000, target Rp3,000,000 | ~35 |
| Premium | Rp45,000,000–50,000,000 | **Target Rp10,000,000 per pilgrim; floor no less than 10% of production cost** | Few |
| Private | Not published | Per-quotation target margin; no published price to measure against | 2–10 |

**Premium carries the highest per-pilgrim margin deliberately.** The market is different, pilgrim numbers are small, and with so few people to spread it across, an unforeseen cost can easily exceed whatever margin a thinner policy would leave.

**Budget is the inverse and is a volume strategy, not a margin strategy.** The publish price is compressed as far as it will go in order to chase quantity. In February 2025 a promo package ran at Rp23,000,000 with IndiGo and a 4-star hotel, was planned at a projected margin of Rp900,000 per pilgrim, and **carried 160 pilgrims** — the company filled most of an aircraft, to the visible surprise of competing operators.

## 2. What the floor is actually protecting — and why it should scale with volume

The Rp2,000,000 floor exists because unforeseen costs have repeatedly eaten thin margins and turned into real losses. But **unforeseen costs are broadly fixed per departure**, not per pilgrim: a delay, a vendor problem, a component that comes in wrong. What protects against them is the **absolute margin buffer on the departure**, not the per-pilgrim figure.

Seen that way, the owner's apparently inconsistent instincts are in fact consistent:

| | Per pilgrim | Pilgrims | Absolute buffer |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Promo Feb 2025 (realised) | Rp1,500,000 | 160 | Rp240,000,000 |
| Promo Feb 2025 (projected) | Rp900,000 | 160 | Rp144,000,000 |
| Premium (at ~20 pilgrims) | Rp10,000,000 | 20 | Rp200,000,000 |
| Standard (floor) | Rp2,000,000 | 35 | Rp70,000,000 |

Promo and premium both land around Rp200 million despite per-pilgrim margins differing by roughly sevenfold. **Standard is the thinnest absolute buffer of the three** — about a third of the others, and lower even than the promo departure's most pessimistic projection.

**Design implication.** Express the floor so it can be defined in any of these shapes, and surface which one is in force:

- an **absolute per-pilgrim** amount (current standard behaviour),
- a **percentage of production cost** (premium's stated minimum of 10%),
- an **absolute departure-level buffer**, which is closer to what the policy is really defending,
- or **the greater of** any combination of the above.

Show both figures wherever margin is displayed: margin per pilgrim **and** total departure margin. A departure clearing the per-pilgrim floor on very few pilgrims may still be carrying an uncomfortably small absolute buffer, and today nothing surfaces that.

## 3. Promo margins are a range, not a point

The February 2025 departure was planned at Rp900,000 per pilgrim and realised Rp1,500,000 — a 67% improvement, caused entirely by securing an IndiGo fare around Rp9,000,000 and cheap low-season hotel rates.

That variance is not noise; it is the nature of the product. A promo package's economics depend on whether cheap inventory is actually secured, and that is not known when the package is planned and marketed.

**For budget/promo tier, present margin as three scenarios rather than one number** — pessimistic, expected and optimistic — driven by the plausible range on the ticket and hotel rates. A single point estimate implies a precision that does not exist and makes a genuinely sound bet look either reckless or safer than it is.

## 4. Season is a strategy input, not just a price adjustment

February is low season, which is why both flights and hotels were cheap and why the promo worked. Promo packages should be **deliberately planned into low season**, and they require long lead time — the owner notes they must be prepared and marketed well in advance to reach that kind of volume.

Two implications for the engine:

- When a departure is being planned, **surface the season** and its historical effect on ticket and hotel rates, so tier selection is an informed choice rather than a habit.
- Promo departures need a **longer planning horizon** than standard ones. Any lead-time warnings or planning checklists should reflect that a promo announced late cannot reach the volume that makes its thin margin work.
